Introduction

This is the first of four tutorials on the Time Value of Money (TVM). In this first part, we'll start with the basic equation and variables. Subsequent tutorials will add complexity by introducing compound interest, payments, and annuities.

TVM may require you to use some math skills that you haven’t used in a while. Contact your instructor if you need additional help.

By the end of this tutorial, you will:

  • Identify the TVM variables within a given word problem
  • Understand the relationship between variables in the TVM equation
  • Memorize and recall the general form of the TVM equation
  • Perform mathematical operations to solve for the unknown variable in the TVM equation, assuming annual interest

Requirements

To complete this tutorial, you will need:

  • Basic algebra skills, including Order of Operations (PEMDAS) and FOIL.
  • Paper and pencil or pen
  • A calculator (optional: a financial calculator)

TVM: The Basics

Central Concept

One of the central concepts of finance is that a dollar today is worth more than a dollar in the future. Transactions operate on this principle every day.

For example, if you make a purchase with a credit card, interest is added to the bill to compensate the credit card company for lending you money. Conversely, if you deposit money in a savings account, the bank pays you interest to compensate you for lending them money.

Introduction

It is important to understand the relationships between the variables that figure into TVM (Time Value of Money). Let's work through a scenario to establish the relationships underlying TVM:

You usually collect customer accounts at the end of the month, but one customer requests an extension. If you forego the payment for a while, you should be compensated for the delay.

What factors would you consider in determining how much the customer should pay at a later date?

Most lenders would want to know:

  • How much is being borrowed?
  • How long will it take the customer to repay the loan?
  • What is the risk that the customer will not be able to pay the account at a later date?
  • What else could your company do with the money if the customer paid now?
  • If the loan is long enough, will inflation further erode the value of tomorrow's dollar?

TVM: The Basics

Equation

To review:

  • We calculated the 1st year of interest earned by multiplying the interest rate, 10%, times the 1st year starting principle, $100.
    ($100 * 10%) = $10
  • We calculated the 2nd year of interest earned by finding 10% of the 2nd year starting principal, $110.
    ($110 * 10%) = $11
  • We added the 1st and 2nd year interest to the principal of $100.
    ($100 + $10 + $11) = $121

In other words, we calculated the interest earned in each individual year and added all the interest payments back to the principal to find the Future Value (FV).

9

TVM: The Basics

Equation

Although calculating the interest for each individual year and adding it to the principle does produce the correct answer, what would you do if asked for the future value after 100 years? You could create a spreadsheet of formulas with 100 interest payment calculations. However, its time consuming and fortunately, unncessary.

Instead, we'll derive a general equation that will allow you to solve for Future Value (FV), Present Value (PV), Interest rate (i), or number of years (n).

Let's look back at our previous calculations:

  1. Figure out the 1st year of interest by finding 10% of $100.
    (100 * 0.10) = 10
  2. Figure out the 2nd year of interest by finding 10% of $110.
    (110 * 0.10) = 11
  3. Add the 1st and 2nd year interest to the principal of 100.
    100 + 10 + 11 = $121
  4. Now look at these steps as one equation.
  5. Let's replace the numbers with variables to make it easier to manipulate the equation.

10

TVM: The Basics

Equation

Now that we have variables in place of numbers, its just a matter of simplifying the formula and grouping like variables.

[(PV + i)] + [(PV * i) + PV] * i + PV

  1. Start with the parenthesis by multiplying the variables within.
    PVi + [PVi + PV] * i + PV
  2. Distribute the multiplication to the variables within the middle bracket.
    PVi + PVi2 + PVi + PV
  3. Group like variables.
    PVi + PVi2 + PVi + PV ⟶ PVi2 + 2PVi + PV
  4. Use the distributive property to separate the like term, PV.
    PV i2 + 2PV i + PV ⟶ PV (i2 + 2i + 1)

We could continue to derive the equation for 3, 10, 100 years. After a lot of algebraic manipulation, the outcome would be the same. Its not so important that you're able to repeat the derivation, but that you understand the equation can be derived from individual calculations.
