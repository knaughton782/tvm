TVM: The Basics

Introduction

One of the central concepts of finance is that a dollar today is worth more than a dollar in the future. Businesses and transactions operate on this principle every day.

For example, if you make a purchase with a credit card, interest is added to the bill to compensate the credit card company for lending you money. Conversely, if you deposit money in a savings account, the bank pays you interest to compensate you for lending them money.

Present Value (PV)

How much is being borrowed? Present Value (PV) is the value in today's terms.

Time (n)

How long before the customer pays? Time (n) is the measure of time in years.

Interest (i)

What is the risk that the customer will not be able to pay the account at a later date? What else could your company do with the money if the customer paid now? If the loan is long enough, will inflation further erode the value of tomorrow's dollar?

Interest (i) is the rate of return, compensating for risk and opportunity cost.

In other words, we calculated the interest earned in each individual year and added all the interest payments back to the principal to find the Future Value (FV).

Although, this method does produce the correct answer, what would you do if asked for the future value after 100 years?

You could create a spreadsheet of formulas with 100 interest payment calculations. However, its time consuming and fortunately, unncessary.

Instead, we'll derive a general equation that will allow you to solve for Future Value (FV), Present Value (PV), Interest rate (i), or number of years (n).
